Commemorating native typography of the Dutch press in Ceylon



This is a series knowledge generation and exchange projects aimed at exploring the shared cultural heritage between Sri Lanka and The Netherlands in areas of typography, printing, graphic design, and type design. This project highlights the Sinhala and Tamil type-making and typography work by the Dutch Press in Ceylon in the 18th century.

Sri Lanka and The Netherlands share a rich cultural heritage, with the introduction of printing in local languages one of the most influential aspects of the Dutch occupancy. The Dutch Government Press of Ceylon (circa 1730–1796), was the first enterprise to print in Sinhala, in 1737, marking the inception of Sinhala typography and printing on the island.

The introduction of native language printing had a profound effect on the people of Ceylon in the coming years, and paved the way for a transformation in the local society. New kinds of literature being printed in Sinhala made the language accessible to the large swathes of the population.
